# Middle Eastern Music Traditions

## Maqam System

The maqam (مقام) system is a melodic mode system governing Arabic, Turkish, and Persian classical music. Key principles:

- **Jins (أجناس)**: Tetrachords that combine to form a maqam. Each maqam = lower jins + upper jins, sometimes with a linking note.
- **Sayr (سير)**: The melodic path — not just a scale but a prescribed way of moving through the notes, with characteristic phrases, emphasis notes, and cadential patterns.
- **Quarter-tones**: Many maqamat include intervals of approximately 150 cents (3/4 tone) or 350 cents, which fall between Western semitones.

## Core Maqamat

| Maqam | Character | Quarter-tones? |
|---|---|---|
| Rast | Joyful, open | Yes (3rd, 7th) |
| Bayati | Warm, earthy | Yes (2nd) |
| Hijaz | Evocative, dramatic | No (12-EDO approx.) |
| Kurd | Melancholic | No (Phrygian-like) |
| Nahawand | Romantic | No (harmonic minor) |

## Ornamentation

- **Trill (زخرفة)**: Rapid alternation between adjacent notes
- **Slide**: Glissando between notes (enabled by fretless oud, ney half-holing)
- **Vibrato**: Subtle pitch oscillation, different in style from Western vibrato

## Structural Patterns

- **Taqsim (تقسيم)**: Improvised solo that explores a maqam, moving from low register to climax and back
- **Wasla (وصلة)**: Suite of pieces in the same maqam, progressing through defined forms
- **Iqa' (إيقاع)**: Rhythmic cycle (comparable to but different from Western meter)

## Counter-Examples

- Turkish pop music often uses Western harmony with maqam melodic inflections
- Modern Arabic pop may use only Hijaz or Nahawand (the most Western-compatible maqamat)
- Israeli/Mizrachi music blends maqam with Western pop structures

## Forbidden Patterns

- Do not call maqam "scales" without qualification — they are modal systems with path rules
- Do not assume all Middle Eastern music uses quarter-tones
- Do not conflate Arabic, Turkish, and Persian systems — they share roots but differ in theory and practice
