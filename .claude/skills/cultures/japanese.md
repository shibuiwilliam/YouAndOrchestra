# Japanese Music Traditions

## Scale Systems

Japanese music uses pentatonic scales organized into two families:

- **In-scale (陰旋法)**: Dark, with semitone intervals. Used in traditional shamisen, koto, and vocal music. Characteristic intervals: m2 + M3 at the start.
- **Yo-scale (陽旋法)**: Bright, without semitones. Used in folk songs (min'yō) and festive music. Equivalent to major pentatonic.

Additional scales:
- **Ritsu (律旋法)**: Gagaku (court music) scale. Dignified character.
- **Hirajoshi (平調子)**: Koto tuning; widely used in contemporary Japanese-influenced music.
- **Iwato (岩戸)**: Most dissonant Japanese pentatonic; dark, mysterious quality.

## Ornamentation

- **Yuri (揺り)**: Vibrato through head movement (shakuhachi) or left hand (koto, shamisen)
- **Meri/Kari**: Pitch bending by adjusting embouchure angle (shakuhachi)
- **Oshi (押し)**: String pressing behind the bridge for pitch bends (koto)
- **Sawari (障り)**: Intentional buzzing timbre on shamisen

## Structural Patterns

- **Jo-ha-kyū (序破急)**: Three-part tempo structure (slow introduction → breaking apart → rapid conclusion). Found across Japanese arts (noh, kabuki, gagaku, tea ceremony). Not equivalent to Western ABA form.
- **Ma (間)**: Intentional silence as a structural element. Silence is not "empty" but active.

## Counter-Examples (When NOT to Apply)

- J-pop and anime music typically use Western scales and harmony, not traditional Japanese scales
- Japanese jazz follows jazz conventions, not gagaku structures
- City pop (シティポップ) is Western-influenced funk/soul

## Forbidden Patterns

- Do not reduce all Japanese music to "pentatonic + koto"
- Do not use the term "Oriental scale" — specify the exact scale by name
- Do not conflate Chinese, Korean, and Japanese musical traditions
