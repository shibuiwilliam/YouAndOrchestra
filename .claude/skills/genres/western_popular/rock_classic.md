# Classic Rock — Genre Skill

## Tempo
- Range: 100-150 BPM
- Sweet spot: 120 BPM (driving energy), 130 BPM (uptempo anthem)
- Slower ballads dip to 70-90 BPM but are a subgenre

## Key Preferences
- Guitar-friendly keys: E, A, D, G (open string resonance)
- C major for piano-driven rock
- Mixolydian mode common (flattened 7th gives bluesy edge)

## Iconic Chord Progressions
1. I-IV-V-I (12-bar blues root, ~30%)
2. I-V-vi-IV (anthem progression, ~25%)
3. I-bVII-IV-I (mixolydian cadence, ~20%)
4. vi-IV-I-V (minor-start variant, ~15%)
5. I-IV-bVII-IV (modal rock, ~10%)

## Drum Pattern Family
- Standard rock beat: kick on 1 & 3, snare on 2 & 4
- Hi-hat: straight eighths or quarter notes
- Fills at section boundaries (4-bar or 8-bar)

## Instrumentation
- Core: electric guitar (distortion), electric bass, drum kit
- Common: piano/organ, second guitar (rhythm), vocals (melody role)
- Avoid: orchestral strings, electronic synths, ethnic instruments

## Section Structure
- Intro: 4-8 bars, guitar riff or drum count-in
- Verse: 8-16 bars, moderate dynamics, rhythmic drive
- Chorus: 8 bars, full band, louder, singable melody
- Bridge: 4-8 bars, harmonic departure (often IV or vi key area)
- Solo: 8-16 bars, guitar lead over verse or chorus chords
- Outro: 4-8 bars, fade or final riff statement

## Cliches to AVOID
- Overusing I-V-vi-IV without any harmonic variation
- Generic power chord patterns lacking melodic contour
- Guitar solos that meander without thematic reference
- Sudden tempo changes (rock thrives on steady groove)
