# Celtic Traditional — Genre Skill

## Cultural Background
Irish and Scottish traditional music is a living oral tradition with deep roots in community practice. The music is traditionally learned by ear in social settings (sessions, ceilis). "Celtic" is a modern grouping — practitioners identify with specific national traditions.

## Modal System
- Dorian mode is extremely common (D dorian on fiddle)
- Mixolydian for a "bright" feel
- Major/Ionian for certain tunes
- Very little use of minor (Aeolian)
- Modal interchange within tunes is characteristic

## Dance Forms
- **Reel**: 4/4, fast (120-140 BPM), eighth-note runs
- **Jig**: 6/8, lively (110-130 BPM), triplet feel
- **Slip jig**: 9/8 (3+3+3), graceful (100-120 BPM)
- **Hornpipe**: 4/4, swung/dotted, stately (80-100 BPM)
- **Slow air**: free tempo, highly ornamented, emotional

## Ornamentation
- Rolls: rapid grace-note groups (cut-triplet)
- Crans: uilleann pipe-specific ornament
- Cuts and strikes: single grace notes
- Slides: approaching notes from below
- Ornamentation is integral, not optional

## Instrumentation
- Melody: fiddle, tin whistle, flute, uilleann pipes, concertina
- Accompaniment: guitar (DADGAD tuning), bouzouki, harp
- Rhythm: bodhran (frame drum) — the ONLY traditional percussion
- Avoid: drum kit, piano (though piano accordion is traditional)

## Session Culture
- Tunes played in sets (3 tunes of the same type)
- Unison melody — NO harmony parts in strict tradition
- Everyone plays at the same volume (no soloists over accompaniment)
- Tunes are learned by ear, not from notation

## CRITICAL: What NOT to Do
- Do NOT add drum kit (use bodhran only)
- Do NOT add harmony parts to melody lines (unison is traditional)
- Do NOT use chromatic passing tones (modal, not chromatic)
- Do NOT use heavy reverb (dry, close-mic sound is traditional)
