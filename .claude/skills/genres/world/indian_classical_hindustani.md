# Indian Classical Hindustani — Genre Skill

## Cultural Background
Hindustani classical music is the North Indian classical tradition, one of the world's oldest continuous musical systems. It is fundamentally different from Western music: there is no harmony, no chord progressions, and no fixed compositions in the Western sense. Instead, music is built on raga (melodic framework) and tala (rhythmic cycle).

## Raga System
- A raga is NOT a scale — it is a melodic personality with specific ascending (aroha) and descending (avaroha) patterns
- Ragas have characteristic phrases (pakad) that define their identity
- Many ragas use microtonal inflections (shruti) that fall between 12-EDO pitches
- Time-of-day associations: Yaman (evening), Bhairav (morning), Darbari (late night)

## Tala (Rhythmic Cycle)
- Tintal: 16 beats (4+4+4+4) — most common
- Jhaptal: 10 beats (2+3+2+3)
- Rupak: 7 beats (3+2+2)
- The "sam" (beat 1) is the point of resolution

## Structure
- Alap: slow, unmetered exploration of the raga (no rhythm)
- Jor: pulsed but unmetered development
- Jhala: fast rhythmic climax with drone
- Gat/Bandish: composed section with tabla accompaniment

## Instrumentation
- Melody: sitar, sarod, bansuri (flute), sarangi, vocal
- Rhythm: tabla (paired hand drums)
- Drone: tanpura (4-string drone instrument)
- No harmony instruments in pure form

## CRITICAL: What NOT to Do
- Do NOT add chord progressions — raga music is melodic, not harmonic
- Do NOT use equal temperament for ragas with microtonal intervals (Darbari, Marwa)
- Do NOT use drum kit patterns — tabla has its own vocabulary (bols)
- Do NOT mix ragas — each raga is a complete musical universe
- Do NOT ignore time-of-day associations without explicit fusion context
