# Acoustic Folk — Genre Skill

## Tempo
- Range: 90-130 BPM
- Sweet spot: 100-115 BPM (walking pace, conversational feel)
- Ballads: 90-100 BPM
- Uptempo reels/jigs: 120-130 BPM
- 3/4 time (waltz) is very common alongside 4/4
- Slight tempo flexibility is natural; strict metronomic feel sounds wrong

## Key Preferences
- G major (guitar-friendly, open strings), C major (warm, accessible)
- D major (bright, ringing), E minor (melancholic folk)
- Guitar-friendly keys dominate (open chord shapes: G, C, D, Am, Em)
- Mixolydian mode for Celtic/Irish-influenced folk (flatted 7th)
- Dorian for darker folk ballads (minor with raised 6th)
- Capo use is implied; actual sounding key may differ from chord shapes

## Iconic Chord Progressions (frequency-ranked)
1. I-IV-V-I (simple folk cadence, ~30%)
2. I-V-vi-IV (modern folk-pop, ~20%)
3. i-bVII-bVI-bVII (Mixolydian/modal folk, ~15%)
4. I-vi-IV-V (classic doo-wop adapted, ~10%)
5. i-iv-i-V (minor folk ballad, ~10%)
6. I-IV-I-V (simple two-chord alternation, ~10%)

## Drum Pattern Family
- Default: ballad_brushed
- Brushed snare on 2 and 4 (light, textural)
- Kick: sparse, on beat 1 and occasionally 3
- Hi-hat: closed, gentle 8ths or just on beats
- Many folk tracks have no drums at all; percussion is optional
- Tambourine or shaker as alternative to full kit
- In 3/4 time: kick on 1, light snare on 2 and 3

## Instrumentation Defaults
- Core: acoustic_guitar_steel (strumming/fingerpicking), acoustic_guitar_nylon (classical passages), piano (harmonic support)
- Common additions: flute (melodic counterpoint), harmonica (implied by organ), contrabass or acoustic_bass (warm low end)
- Violin/fiddle for Celtic and Appalachian styles
- Avoid: synths, electric instruments, heavy processing

## Section Structure
- Intro: 4 bars, guitar fingerpicking pattern establishing rhythm and key
- Verse 1: 8-16 bars, storytelling, sparse arrangement (guitar + voice)
- Chorus: 4-8 bars, fuller texture, group sing-along quality
- Verse 2: 8-16 bars, same chords, new lyrics, possibly add instrument
- Chorus: repeat with slight variation (add harmony vocal = add instrument)
- Bridge: 4-8 bars, instrumental break (fiddle/flute solo) or contrasting section
- Final Chorus: full arrangement, possibly key change or added voices
- Outro: 4 bars, return to intro fingerpicking, fade or resolving strum

## Trajectory Patterns
- Storyteller: tension 0.2 -> 0.3 -> 0.5 -> 0.3 -> 0.6 -> 0.2
- Campfire: tension 0.3 -> 0.4 -> 0.5 -> 0.4 (gentle, steady)
- Ballad Build: tension 0.15 -> 0.25 -> 0.4 -> 0.6 -> 0.7 -> 0.2

## Cadences
- Authentic (V-I) for strong phrase endings
- Plagal (IV-I) for gentle, hymn-like resolutions
- Mixolydian cadence (bVII-I) for Celtic/modal endings
- Amen cadence (IV-I) at song end is very common
- Avoid tritone-heavy cadences (too jazzy for the genre)

## Cliches to AVOID
- Overproduction (reverb, compression, layering destroy intimacy)
- Electric instruments or synths (breaks the acoustic aesthetic)
- Complex chord extensions (folk harmony is triadic with occasional 7ths)
- Drum fills or busy percussion (subtlety is essential)
- Overly fast tempos (folk is conversational, not frenetic)
- Perfect quantization (human timing imperfection is a feature)

## Quality Heuristics
- Guitar patterns: fingerpicking (Travis picking, arpeggios) or strumming (down-up patterns)
- Voicings: open position chords with ringing open strings
- Bass: root notes on strong beats, simple walking patterns at most
- Melody: stepwise, singable, within an octave, folk scale (pentatonic common)
- Dynamics: intimate (pp-mf range), crescendo only at emotional peaks
- Texture: start sparse, add instruments gradually across verses
- 3/4 time: waltz feel with emphasis on beat 1, lighter 2 and 3
- Harmonic rhythm: one chord per bar or two bars (unhurried)
- Repetition of chord patterns is expected and desired (supports lyric focus)

## Use Cases
- Singer-songwriter albums
- Campfire and coffeehouse performances
- Celtic/Irish traditional arrangements
- Americana and country-folk crossover
- Acoustic covers and unplugged sessions
