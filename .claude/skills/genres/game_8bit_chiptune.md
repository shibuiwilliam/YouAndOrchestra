# Game 8-bit Chiptune — Genre Skill

## Tempo
- Range: 120-170 BPM
- Sweet spot: 140-160 BPM (energetic action/adventure feel)
- Title screen / menu: 120-130 BPM (more relaxed)
- Boss battle: 160-170 BPM (maximum urgency)
- Straight time only; no swing, no rubato

## Key Preferences
- C major (bright, heroic), G major (adventurous)
- A minor (dungeon/mystery), E minor (dark, dramatic)
- Mixolydian mode for a slightly quirky, playful color
- Key changes are rare; modulation up a half step for final loop is acceptable
- Simple key signatures preferred (few sharps/flats)

## Iconic Chord Progressions (frequency-ranked)
1. I-IV-V-I (simple major, ~30%)
2. i-bVI-bVII-i (minor power, ~25%)
3. I-V-vi-IV (pop-influenced chiptune, ~15%)
4. i-iv-v-i (natural minor, ~15%)
5. I-bVII-IV-I (Mixolydian rock, ~10%)

## Drum Pattern Family
- Default: game_drive_16bit
- Kick: steady quarter notes or on 1 and 3
- Snare/noise channel: on 2 and 4
- Hi-hat: straight 8ths or 16ths (noise channel simulation)
- Fills: simple tom rolls (descending pitch sweeps)
- All percussion is synthesized noise, not sampled drums

## Instrumentation Defaults
- Core: synth_lead_square (melody), synth_lead_saw (harmony/countermelody), synth_bass (bass line)
- Maximum 3-4 simultaneous voices (hardware limitation aesthetic)
- Channel allocation: 2 pulse waves, 1 triangle (bass), 1 noise (drums)
- Avoid: orchestral instruments, pads, acoustic instruments, reverb-heavy sounds

## Section Structure
- Intro: 4 bars, ascending arpeggio or power-up fanfare
- Loop A: 8-16 bars, main theme (catchy, memorable melody)
- Loop B: 8-16 bars, contrasting section (often in relative minor/major)
- Bridge: 4-8 bars, breakdown or builds (reduced voices)
- Loop back: tracks are designed to loop seamlessly
- Total: 32-64 bars before looping (game music repeats indefinitely)

## Trajectory Patterns
- Stage Theme: tension 0.4 -> 0.5 -> 0.6 -> 0.5 (moderate, steady)
- Boss Battle: tension 0.7 -> 0.8 -> 0.9 -> 0.8 (high throughout)
- Title Screen: tension 0.3 -> 0.4 -> 0.3 (gentle, inviting)

## Cadences
- Authentic (V-I) for clear phrase endings
- bVII-I (modal) for a less classical, more game-like feel
- Avoid half cadences (phrases should resolve cleanly for looping)
- Loop point: final chord should transition smoothly back to the first

## Cliches to AVOID
- Complex jazz voicings (chiptune uses simple triads and octaves)
- Reverb or delay effects (8-bit hardware had none)
- More than 4 simultaneous voices (breaks the aesthetic constraint)
- Long sustained notes without vibrato or pitch wobble
- Gradual dynamic changes (chiptune is terraced dynamics: on or off)
- Legato phrasing (staccato and detached articulation are standard)

## Quality Heuristics
- Melody: catchy, singable, within one octave, stepwise with occasional leaps
- Arpeggios: fast broken chords are a signature technique (16th note arpeggios)
- Bass: octave jumps, arpeggiated root-fifth patterns, active movement
- Countermelody: simple harmony a 3rd or 6th above/below the melody
- Velocity: mostly uniform (90-110 range); dynamics are textural, not velocity-based
- Note density: high; 16th notes are common for runs and fills
- Pitch bends: rapid slides between notes for expressive effect
- Duty cycle variety: alternate between square wave timbres for tonal contrast

## Use Cases
- Retro game soundtracks (platformer, RPG, shooter)
- Indie game music
- Chiptune albums and live performances
- Nostalgic/retro content creation
- Game jam prototypes
