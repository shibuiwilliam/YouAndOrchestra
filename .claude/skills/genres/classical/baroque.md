# Baroque — Genre Skill

## Tempo
- Wide range depending on form: 50 BPM (adagio) to 140 BPM (allegro vivace)
- Dance movements have specific tempi (sarabande ~60, gigue ~120)
- Tempo is steady — no romantic rubato

## Key Preferences
- Sharp keys: D, G, A (bright, trumpet-friendly)
- Flat keys: F, Bb (woodwind-friendly)
- C major for ceremonial works
- Minor keys for pathos (D minor, G minor, C minor)

## Iconic Chord Progressions
1. I-V-I-IV-V-I (basic tonal framework, ~30%)
2. i-iv-V-i (minor cadential, ~25%)
3. Circle of fifths: I-IV-vii-iii-vi-ii-V-I (~20%)
4. i-VII-III-iv-V-i (minor with modal inflections, ~15%)
5. Lament bass: i-VII-VI-V (descending tetrachord, ~10%)

## Counterpoint
- Baroque is defined by independent melodic lines
- 2-4 voices is typical; each voice has melodic independence
- Avoid parallel fifths and octaves strictly
- Imitation: subject enters in different voices at different times
- Species counterpoint principles apply

## Basso Continuo
- Harpsichord/organ + cello/bass provide harmonic foundation
- Figured bass notation implies chord realizations
- Bass line walks steadily, often in eighth notes

## Dynamics
- Terraced dynamics: sudden shifts between loud and soft
- NO crescendo/decrescendo (that is Classical/Romantic era)
- Echo effects: loud phrase repeated softly

## Instrumentation
- Core: harpsichord (continuo), violin, cello
- Common: flute, oboe, trumpet (in D), contrabass
- Avoid: piano (anachronistic), percussion kits, electric instruments

## Section Structure (Suite movements)
- Allemande: moderate 4/4, flowing eighth notes
- Courante: quick 3/4, running passages
- Sarabande: slow 3/4, emphasis on beat 2
- Gigue: fast 6/8 or 12/8, compound meter
- Prelude-Fugue pair is the intellectual pinnacle

## Cliches to AVOID
- Romantic-era dynamics (no gradual crescendo)
- Rubato or flexible tempo
- Thick homophonic textures (keep voices independent)
- Modern instrument timbres
